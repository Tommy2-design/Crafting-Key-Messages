Key Concepts — How to Craft Key Messages

1 · The umbrella (big) message

Across marketing, health and political communication, research converges on one idea: a clear, well-framed big message drives strategic impact. It is not everything you could say — it is the essential idea your audience should remember and act on. It is supported by 2–3 sub-key messages, and your tactics are later built from those sub-key messages.

2 · Message framing (Tsai, 2007 — the MFBC model)

Messages can use positive framing (what the audience gains) or negative framing (what they stand to lose). Neither is universally better — effectiveness depends on who receives the message, defined by three traits:

Audience traitProfileBest framing
Self-construal: Independent"I am my own person" — personal goals & autonomy✚ Positive
Self-construal: Interdependent"I belong to my group" — family & community (common in Vietnam)✖ Negative
Involvement: HighHigh-stakes purchase — health, finance, family✖ Negative
Involvement: LowLow stakes, casual, easy to replace✚ Positive
Knowledge: High (expert)Evaluates on facts & substance, not presentation📋 Facts only
Knowledge: Low (novice)New to the category — responds to tone & feeling✚ Positive

3 · How to build the big message

  • Profile your audience — measure self-construal (I vs. we), involvement (stakes felt) and product knowledge (expert vs. novice).
  • Choose your frame — independent + low involvement + low knowledge → positive; interdependent + high involvement → negative; high knowledge → facts only.
  • Write the big message — one emotional idea anchoring all executions. Positive = what they gain. Negative = what they lose. Expert = what is proven.
